Malaysia Legal Context for Contract Review
This document provides jurisdiction-specific legal context for reviewing contracts governed by Malaysian law.
Use this as a reference when a contract specifies Malaysia as the governing jurisdiction, or when parties are Malaysian entities.

1. Governing Law & Jurisdiction
What to check

Contracts between Malaysian parties should specify Malaysian law as governing law.
Dispute resolution should reference Malaysian courts or Asian International Arbitration Centre (AIAC).

Red flags

Governing law set to a foreign jurisdiction (e.g. Singapore, UK, Cayman Islands) with no explanation — this makes enforcement harder and more expensive for the Malaysian party.
No governing law clause at all — creates ambiguity on which country's courts handle disputes.
Arbitration seat set outside Malaysia without justification — adds significant cost for a local SME.

Relevant law

Contracts Act 1950 — governs what makes a contract valid and enforceable in Malaysia.
Civil Law Act 1956 — allows Malaysian courts to apply English common law principles where Malaysian statute is silent.


2. Personal Data Protection Act 2010 (PDPA)
Applies when
Any contract involving the collection, processing, or transfer of personal data of Malaysian individuals — employment contracts, vendor agreements, SaaS agreements, customer data processing agreements.
Mandatory obligations to look for

Notice and choice — the contract must state what personal data is collected and for what purpose.
Data security — reasonable security standards must be stated.
Retention — personal data should not be kept longer than necessary.
Transfer outside Malaysia — data cannot be transferred to countries not approved by the Minister unless consent is obtained.

Red flags

No data protection clause at all in a contract involving personal data handling.
Clause allows transfer of personal data to any country without restriction.
No mention of data breach notification obligations.
Data retention period is "indefinite" or unspecified.
Vendor contract gives the vendor rights to use client data for their own purposes.

Note for employers
Employment contracts must comply with PDPA for employee personal data — payroll data, medical records, performance data.

Applies to
Employees earning RM4,000/month or below, or those in manual labour roles regardless of salary (First Schedule). Amendments effective 2023 extended some protections further.

Red flags in employment contracts

Annual leave below the statutory minimum.
Maternity leave stated as 60 days — this is the old provision, 98 days is now required.
Notice period shorter than statutory minimum.
No mention of overtime entitlement for covered employees.
Probation period exceeding 6 months without justification — not illegal but unusual and worth flagging.
Contract states "employment at will" — this concept does not exist in Malaysian law; termination requires cause or proper notice.

Non-compete clauses
Malaysian courts have historically been reluctant to enforce overbroad non-compete clauses post-employment. A clause is more likely enforceable if:

Duration is 12 months or less
Geographic scope is reasonable (e.g. Klang Valley, not "worldwide")
It protects a legitimate business interest (not just restricting competition generally)

Flag any non-compete exceeding 12 months duration or with nationwide/global scope.

4. Contracts Act 1950
Key principles relevant to contract review
Valid contract requires:

Offer and acceptance
Consideration (something of value exchanged by both parties)
Free consent (no duress, fraud, misrepresentation, undue influence)
Lawful object
Parties must be competent (age 18+, sound mind)

Red flags

Consideration is stated as "RM1" or nominal — technically valid but signals a potentially unbalanced agreement.
One party has the unilateral right to vary terms — may constitute lack of free consent.
Penalty clauses that are extravagant or unconscionable — Malaysian courts may refuse to enforce these.
Contract signed by someone without authority to bind the company — check for proper authorisation clauses.


5. Stamp Duty Act 1949
Why it matters
Unstamped contracts are not admissible as evidence in Malaysian courts. This means if a dispute arises, an unstamped contract cannot be used to prove the agreement.

6. Limitation Act 1953
Why it matters
This sets the time limit within which a party can bring a legal claim. After this period, the right to sue is lost.
Key limitation periods
Type of claimLimitation periodBreach of contract6 years from date of breachTort (negligence, fraud)6 yearsRecovery of land12 yearsClaim against government36 months
Red flags

Contract attempts to shorten the limitation period to less than 6 years — Malaysian courts may not uphold this.
No date of execution on the contract — limitation period cannot be calculated.


7. Consumer Protection Act 1999
Applies when
One party is a consumer (individual purchasing goods/services for personal use, not business).
Key protections

Unfair contract terms can be voided.
Implied warranties on quality of goods and services cannot be excluded for consumer contracts.
No false or misleading representations.

Red flags in consumer-facing contracts

"All sales final, no refunds under any circumstances" — may be unenforceable for defective goods.
Exclusion of all liability for the supplier — courts may strike this down.
Terms and conditions that are unreasonably one-sided against the consumer.
